Module 2: Foundations in Biology
2.1.1 Cell Structure
Use of microscopy (light, TEM, SEM) to observe different cell types; appreciation of image differences between microscope types
Preparation and examination of microscope slides; use of eyepiece graticule and stage micrometer (PAG1)
Use of staining (differential staining to identify cell components and types) (PAG1)
Biological drawings from light microscope observations (PAG1)
Magnification formula: magnification = size of image / size of real object
Difference between magnification and resolution across microscope types
Ultrastructure of eukaryotic cells: nucleus, nucleolus, nuclear envelope, rough ER, smooth ER, Golgi apparatus, ribosomes, mitochondria, lysosomes, chloroplasts, plasma membrane, centrioles, cell wall, flagella, cilia — and their functions
Interpretation of photomicrographs (TEM and SEM images)
Interrelationship between organelles in protein production and secretion
Importance of the cytoskeleton (mechanical strength, intracellular transport, cell movement)
Similarities and differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells (PAG1)
2.1.2 Biological Molecules
Water: hydrogen bonding between water molecules; properties of water (solvent, transport medium, coolant, habitat) with examples from prokaryotes and eukaryotes
Monomers and polymers: condensation and hydrolysis reactions
Chemical elements: C, H, O (carbohydrates/lipids); C, H, O, N, S (proteins); C, H, O, N, P (nucleic acids)
Monosaccharides: ring structure of glucose (α and β forms); ribose as pentose; hexose vs pentose
Disaccharides: sucrose, lactose, maltose; glycosidic bond formation and breakage
Polysaccharides: structure of starch (amylose, amylopectin), glycogen, cellulose; structure-function relationships
Lipids: structure of triglycerides and phospholipids; saturated/unsaturated fatty acids; ester bond formation/breakage; functions of triglycerides, phospholipids and cholesterol (hydrophobic/hydrophilic regions, energy content)
Amino acids: general structure; peptide bond formation/breakage
Protein structure: primary, secondary, tertiary, quaternary; hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic/hydrophilic interactions, disulfide bonds, ionic bonds
Globular proteins: haemoglobin (conjugated protein with prosthetic group), a named enzyme, insulin (PAG10)
Fibrous proteins: collagen, keratin, elastin (functions, no structural detail required)
Inorganic ions: Ca²⁺, Na⁺, K⁺, H⁺, NH₄⁺, NO₃⁻, HCO₃⁻, Cl⁻, PO₄³⁻, OH⁻
Biochemical tests (PAG9): biuret (proteins), Benedict's (reducing/non-reducing sugars), iodine (starch), emulsion (lipids)
Quantitative methods: colorimetry to determine concentration (PAG5)
Chromatography (PAG6): paper and TLC; Rf value = distance moved by solute / distance moved by solvent; separation of proteins, carbohydrates, vitamins, nucleic acids
2.1.3 Nucleotides and Nucleic Acids
Structure of nucleotides (DNA vs RNA differences, purines/pyrimidines, pentose sugar type)
Polynucleotide synthesis/breakdown via phosphodiester bonds
Structure of ADP and ATP (ribose, adenine, inorganic phosphates)
Structure of DNA: complementary base pairing (A–T, G–C), hydrogen bonding, antiparallel strands, double helix (PAG9)
DNA purification by precipitation (practical)
Semi-conservative replication: roles of helicase and DNA polymerase; importance of accuracy; random spontaneous mutations
The genetic code: triplet, non-overlapping, degenerate, universal; gene → amino acid sequence → polypeptide
Transcription and translation: roles of RNA polymerase, mRNA, tRNA, rRNA
2.1.4 Enzymes
Role of enzymes in metabolism (intracellular and extracellular); catalase (intracellular), amylase (extracellular)
Mechanism of action: tertiary structure, specificity, active site, lock-and-key hypothesis, induced-fit hypothesis, enzyme-substrate complex, enzyme-product complex, lowering activation energy
Effects of pH, temperature, enzyme concentration, substrate concentration on activity; Q₁₀ = R₂/R₁ (PAG4)
Coenzymes (vitamins as source) and cofactors (Cl⁻ for amylase) (PAG4)
Inhibitors: competitive, non-competitive, reversible, non-reversible; end-product inhibition (PAG4)
2.1.5 Biological Membranes
Roles of membranes: partially permeable barriers, sites of chemical reactions, sites of cell signalling
Fluid mosaic model: phospholipids, cholesterol, glycolipids, proteins, glycoproteins; membrane-bound receptors for hormones and drugs
Factors affecting membrane structure and permeability (temperature, solvents) (PAG5, PAG8)
Movement across membranes: diffusion, facilitated diffusion (passive); active transport, endocytosis, exocytosis (require ATP) (PAG8)
Osmosis: water potential gradient across partially permeable membrane; effects on plant and animal cells (PAG8)
2.1.6 Cell Division, Cell Diversity and Cellular Organisation
Cell cycle: interphase (G₁, S, G₂), mitosis, cytokinesis → genetically identical cells; checkpoints
Mitosis stages: changes in nuclear envelope, chromosomes, chromatids, centromere, centrioles, spindle fibres, cell membrane; examination of stained sections/squashes (PAG1)
Significance of mitosis: growth, tissue repair, asexual reproduction
Meiosis: production of haploid cells; genetic variation via independent assortment and crossing over; main stages (interphase through telophase 2; homologous chromosomes) (PAG1)
Cell specialisation: erythrocytes, neutrophils, squamous epithelial, ciliated epithelial, sperm, palisade, root hair, guard cells (PAG1)
Organisation: cells → tissues → organs → organ systems; tissue examples (squamous/ciliated epithelia, cartilage, muscle, xylem, phloem)
Stem cells: features and differentiation; renewing source of undifferentiated cells; production of erythrocytes and neutrophils as examples; therapeutic potential and bioethical issues
Module 3: Exchange and Transport
3.1.1 Exchange Surfaces
Need for exchange surfaces and transport systems in multicellular organisms (SA:V ratio)
Features of exchange surfaces: large surface area, thin barrier, concentration/pressure gradient, ventilation/blood supply
Gas exchange in single-celled organisms, insects (tracheal system), fish (gills — countercurrent), amphibians (skin and lungs)
Mammalian gas exchange: structure of the lungs (trachea, bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli); ventilation mechanism; adaptations for efficient gas exchange (PAG2)
Dissection of gas exchange and/or transport system of an organism (PAG3)
3.1.2 Transport in Animals
Need for a transport system in multicellular animals
Structure of the mammalian circulatory system: double circulation; structure of the heart (atria, ventricles, valves, aorta, vena cava, pulmonary artery/vein, coronary arteries, septum)
The cardiac cycle: atrial systole, ventricular systole, diastole; pressure and volume changes; heart sounds
Cardiac output = heart rate × stroke volume
Control of heart rate: myogenic nature; sinoatrial node (SAN), atrioventricular node (AVN), bundle of His, Purkinje fibres; role of medulla oblongata, sympathetic/parasympathetic nerves, chemoreceptors, baroreceptors
Blood vessels: structure and function of arteries, arterioles, capillaries, venules, veins; adaptations for function
Blood and tissue fluid: formation of tissue fluid; lymph; composition of blood (plasma, erythrocytes, leucocytes, platelets)
Haemoglobin and oxygen transport: oxygen dissociation curves; cooperative binding; Bohr effect; foetal haemoglobin (PAG10)
Dissection of the mammalian heart (PAG3)
3.1.3 Transport in Plants
Structure of xylem and phloem: xylem vessels, tracheids, sieve tube elements, companion cells; adaptations for function
Water transport: water uptake by roots (osmosis, apoplast/symplast/vacuolar pathways); transpiration stream; cohesion-tension theory; adhesion; role of root pressure
Transpiration: definition; factors affecting transpiration rate (light, temperature, humidity, wind); use of a potometer (PAG7)
Xerophytic and hydrophytic adaptations
Translocation: mass flow hypothesis (pressure-flow); evidence for and against; loading and unloading of sucrose at source and sink; role of companion cells and active transport (PAG7)
Module 4: Biodiversity, Evolution and Disease
4.1.1 Communicable Diseases, Disease Prevention and the Immune System
Communicable diseases: diseases caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, protoctista, fungi); transmission methods (direct contact, droplet infection, vectors, contaminated food/water)
Specific examples: HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis (TB), malaria, ring rot (plant), tobacco mosaic virus (TMV)
Plant defences: physical barriers (cellulose cell wall, waxy cuticle, bark); chemical defences (tannins, alkaloids); other responses (callose, lignin deposition, tylose formation)
Non-specific animal defences: barrier defences (skin, mucous membranes, lysozyme); inflammation; phagocytosis (neutrophils, macrophages) — recognition, engulfing, destruction (PAG11)
Specific immune response:
Antigens and the immune response; self vs non-self
T lymphocytes: T helper, T killer, T regulatory (suppressor); role of antigen presentation; MHC proteins
B lymphocytes: clonal selection and expansion; plasma cells and memory cells
Antibody structure and function (variable/constant regions, antigen-antibody complex); agglutination, neutralisation, opsonisation
Cell-mediated vs humoral immunity
Primary and secondary immune response
Types of immunity: active (natural/artificial), passive (natural/artificial)
Vaccination: herd immunity; ring vaccination; benefits and risks; antigenic variation
Antibiotics: action of antibiotics on bacteria (not viruses); antibiotic resistance; importance of responsible use; MRSA
Personalised medicine: monoclonal antibodies (production and uses in diagnosis and treatment)
4.2.1 Biodiversity
Biodiversity: definitions at different levels (habitat, species, genetic)
Sampling techniques: random and non-random sampling; quadrats, transects (belt and line), mark-release-recapture; reliability of results
Simpson's Index of Diversity: D = 1 − Σ(n/N)²
Factors affecting biodiversity: human activities (deforestation, agriculture, pollution, climate change); conservation measures
Maintaining biodiversity: reasons for maintaining biodiversity (ecological, economic, aesthetic, ethical); in situ conservation (nature reserves, wildlife corridors, SSSIs); ex situ conservation (zoos, seed banks, botanical gardens); international agreements (CITES, Rio Convention, Countryside Stewardship)
4.2.2 Classification and Evolution
Classification: taxonomic hierarchy (domain, kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, species); binomial naming system
Three-domain system: Archaea, Bacteria, Eukarya (based on rRNA differences)
Phylogeny: evolutionary relationships; phylogenetic trees/cladograms
Evidence for evolution: fossil record; comparative anatomy (homologous structures); molecular comparisons (DNA, amino acid sequences)
Natural selection: variation, overproduction, struggle for existence, survival of the fittest, reproduction; adaptations (anatomical, physiological, behavioural)
Types of selection: stabilising, directional, disruptive
Genetic drift and speciation: allopatric and sympatric speciation; reproductive isolation; role of genetic drift in small populations
Artificial selection: selective breeding in agriculture; comparison with natural selection
Module 5: Communication, Homeostasis and Energy
5.1.1 Communication and Homeostasis
Need for communication systems: internal and external environment changes; coordination
Cell signalling: neuronal (electrical), hormonal (chemical); cell-surface receptors; complementary shape of signal molecule and receptor
Homeostasis: maintaining a stable internal environment; importance of maintaining body temperature, blood glucose, blood water potential
Negative feedback: receptor → communication pathway → effector → response → correction; example loops
Positive feedback: examples (e.g. oxytocin during labour, blood clotting); destabilising effect
Ectotherms and endotherms: thermoregulation mechanisms; behavioural and physiological responses; role of the hypothalamus
5.1.2 Excretion as an Example of Homeostatic Control
Excretion: definition; roles of the liver and kidneys
The liver: structure (hepatocytes, sinusoids, Kupffer cells, bile canaliculi, hepatic artery, hepatic portal vein, hepatic vein); functions (detoxification, deamination of amino acids → urea via ornithine cycle)
The kidney: gross structure (cortex, medulla, pelvis, ureter, renal artery/vein)
Nephron structure and function: Bowman's capsule (ultrafiltration), proximal convoluted tubule (selective reabsorption), loop of Henle (countercurrent multiplier, water reabsorption), distal convoluted tubule, collecting duct
Osmoregulation: role of ADH; hypothalamus osmoreceptors; negative feedback control of water potential
Kidney failure: causes; dialysis (haemodialysis, peritoneal); kidney transplant; comparison of treatments
Testing for glucose and protein in urine
5.1.3 Neuronal Communication
Sensory receptors: transducers; generator potentials; Pacinian corpuscle as an example (pressure deformation → sodium ion channels open)
Neurones: sensory, relay, motor; structure (cell body, dendrites, axon, myelin sheath, Schwann cells, nodes of Ranvier)
Resting potential: maintained by Na⁺/K⁺ pump; distribution of ions; polarisation (~−70 mV)
Action potential: depolarisation, repolarisation, hyperpolarisation; threshold; role of voltage-gated sodium and potassium channels; all-or-nothing principle; refractory period
Nerve impulse transmission: local currents; saltatory conduction in myelinated neurones; factors affecting speed (myelination, axon diameter, temperature)
Synapses: structure (presynaptic membrane, synaptic cleft, postsynaptic membrane); cholinergic synapse (acetylcholine, calcium ions, vesicle fusion, receptor binding, acetylcholinesterase)
Roles of synapses: unidirectionality, summation (spatial and temporal), divergence, convergence
Effects of drugs on synaptic transmission (agonists, antagonists)
5.1.4 Hormonal Communication
Endocrine system: endocrine glands (adrenal glands, pancreas, thyroid, ovaries, testes); hormones transported in blood
Adrenal glands: structure (cortex, medulla); hormones (cortisol, aldosterone from cortex; adrenaline from medulla); fight-or-flight response
Blood glucose regulation: role of the pancreas (islets of Langerhans: α cells — glucagon, β cells — insulin)
Insulin: secretion in response to high blood glucose; mechanism (glycogenesis, increased glucose uptake, increased respiration rate); second messenger model (cAMP)
Glucagon: secretion in response to low blood glucose; mechanism (glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis)
Negative feedback in blood glucose control
Diabetes mellitus: Type 1 (autoimmune destruction of β cells; insulin injections) vs Type 2 (insulin resistance; lifestyle management); potential use of stem cells
5.1.5 Plant and Animal Responses
Plant responses: tropisms (phototropism, geotropism, thigmotropism); role of auxins (IAA) in phototropism and gravitropism; experimental evidence; apical dominance; other plant hormones (gibberellins, ABA, ethylene) and commercial uses
Animal responses: nervous vs hormonal coordination; the fight-or-flight response; role of the medulla oblongata; reflexes (reflex arc)
Muscle structure and contraction: gross structure of skeletal muscle; ultrastructure of a muscle fibre (myofibrils, sarcomere, actin, myosin, tropomyosin, troponin); sliding filament model; role of calcium ions, ATP and ATPase; neuromuscular junction
Slow-twitch and fast-twitch muscle fibres
5.2.1 Photosynthesis
Overview: light energy → chemical energy; equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
Chloroplast structure: thylakoids (grana, intergranal lamellae), stroma; adaptations for function
Light-dependent reactions (thylakoid membranes):
Photosystems I and II; photophosphorylation (cyclic and non-cyclic)
Photolysis of water; generation of ATP and reduced NADP (NADPH); oxygen as a by-product
Electron transport chain; chemiosmosis
Light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle, stroma):
Carbon fixation: CO₂ + RuBP → 2× GP (catalysed by RuBisCO)
Reduction of GP → TP (triose phosphate) using ATP and reduced NADP
Regeneration of RuBP from TP
TP as the starting material for synthesis of glucose and other organic molecules
Factors affecting photosynthesis: light intensity, CO₂ concentration, temperature; limiting factors; practical investigations (PAG10)
Chromatography of photosynthetic pigments (PAG6)
5.2.2 Respiration
Overview: the need for cellular respiration; ATP as the universal energy currency
Glycolysis (cytoplasm): glucose (6C) → 2× pyruvate (3C); net gain of 2 ATP and 2 reduced NAD; phosphorylation steps
Link reaction (mitochondrial matrix): pyruvate → acetyl CoA + CO₂; reduction of NAD
Krebs cycle (mitochondrial matrix): acetyl CoA + oxaloacetate (4C) → citrate (6C) → regeneration of oxaloacetate; per turn: 2 CO₂, 1 ATP (substrate-level phosphorylation), 3 reduced NAD, 1 reduced FAD
Oxidative phosphorylation (inner mitochondrial membrane):
Electron transport chain; hydrogen carriers (NADH, FADH₂)
Chemiosmosis; role of ATP synthase; proton motive force
Oxygen as the final electron acceptor → water
Theoretical maximum yield of ATP per glucose molecule (~30–38 ATP)
Anaerobic respiration: fermentation in animals (pyruvate → lactate) and in yeast/plants (pyruvate → ethanol + CO₂); oxygen debt
Respiratory substrates: lipids, proteins, carbohydrates; respiratory quotient (RQ) = CO₂ produced / O₂ consumed
Use of a respirometer (PAG10)
Module 6: Genetics, Evolution and Ecosystems
6.1.1 Cellular Control
Gene mutations: types (substitution, insertion, deletion); effects on protein structure and function (silent, missense, nonsense, frameshift)
Gene expression: regulatory sequences; promoters; transcription factors
Lac operon (E. coli) as an example of gene regulation in prokaryotes: structural genes, operator, promoter, regulatory gene, repressor protein; role of inducer (lactose/allolactose)
Homeobox genes (Hox genes): role in controlling body plan development; highly conserved across species; apoptosis (programmed cell death) in development
Epigenetics: gene expression changes without DNA sequence alteration; DNA methylation; histone modification; environmental influences
6.1.2 Patterns of Inheritance
Genetic terminology: gene, allele, locus, genotype, phenotype, dominant, recessive, codominant, multiple alleles, sex-linked, autosomal, homozygous, heterozygous, carrier
Monohybrid crosses: genetic diagrams, Punnett squares, predicted ratios
Dihybrid crosses: independent assortment; predicted ratios (9:3:3:1 etc.)
Codominance and multiple alleles: ABO blood group system
Sex linkage: X-linked inheritance (e.g. haemophilia, colour blindness)
Autosomal linkage: linked genes on the same chromosome; effect on phenotypic ratios
Epistasis: interaction between gene loci affecting phenotype
Chi-squared (χ²) test: testing the significance of differences between observed and expected results; degrees of freedom; critical values; null hypothesis
Continuous and discontinuous variation: polygenic inheritance; environmental influences; gene-environment interactions
6.1.3 Manipulating Genomes
DNA sequencing: principles of whole-genome sequencing; the Human Genome Project
Bioinformatics and genomics: comparison of genomes and proteomes; uses in medicine, agriculture, evolutionary biology
Recombinant DNA technology: restriction enzymes (recognition sequences, sticky/blunt ends); DNA ligase; vectors (plasmids, bacteriophages); transformation
Polymerase chain reaction (PCR): stages (denaturation, annealing, extension); role of primers and Taq polymerase
Gel electrophoresis: separation of DNA fragments by size; use of restriction enzymes; DNA profiling/fingerprinting; applications (forensics, paternity, medical diagnosis)
Genetic engineering: production of transformed organisms; examples (insulin production by bacteria, golden rice, herbicide-resistant crops)
Gene therapy: somatic vs germ-line; potential and challenges
Ethical, moral and social issues surrounding genetic technologies: GM organisms, genetic screening, gene therapy, genetic databases
6.2.1 Cloning and Biotechnology
Natural clones: vegetative propagation in plants; identical twins in animals
Artificial cloning in plants: micropropagation (tissue culture) using callus and growth media
Artificial cloning in animals: somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT); uses of cloned animals
Arguments for and against cloning
Biotechnology: use of microorganisms in industry
Fermenters/bioreactors: batch vs continuous culture; aseptic techniques; monitoring and controlling conditions (temperature, pH, oxygen, nutrients)
Immobilised enzymes: methods (alginate beads, surface attachment); advantages in industrial processes
Examples: production of penicillin, insulin, food products (bread, beer, yoghurt, Quorn/mycoprotein)
6.3.1 Ecosystems
Ecosystem: community of organisms interacting with each other and their abiotic environment
Biotic and abiotic factors
Levels of organisation: individual → population → community → ecosystem → biome
Energy transfer through ecosystems:
Producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, tertiary consumers, decomposers
Food chains, food webs, trophic levels
Gross primary productivity (GPP), net primary productivity (NPP): NPP = GPP − R
Efficiency of energy transfer between trophic levels (~10%); calculations
Nutrient cycling:
Carbon cycle: photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, decomposition, fossilisation
Nitrogen cycle: nitrogen fixation (free-living and mutualistic bacteria, e.g. Rhizobium), nitrification, denitrification, ammonification; role of decomposers
Succession: primary and secondary succession; pioneer species → climax community; deflected succession (plagioclimax)
6.3.2 Populations and Sustainability
Population growth: S-shaped (sigmoid) growth curves; lag, log (exponential), stationary phases; carrying capacity
Factors affecting population size: birth rate, death rate, immigration, emigration; density-dependent and density-independent factors; predator-prey relationships
Competition: intraspecific and interspecific; competitive exclusion principle; niche concept
Sustainability and conservation:
Sustainable management of resources (timber production — coppicing, selective felling, replanting; fish stocks — quotas, mesh sizes, breeding seasons)
Balancing human needs with conservation
The impact of human activities on ecosystems: deforestation, desertification, pollution, overharvesting, climate change
The Galapagos Islands and/or other named examples of conservation management
